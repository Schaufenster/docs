Account management is about staying ahead of issues, building trust, and helping customers get more value from Egora over time. The KAMs who perform best don't wait for problems to surface — they check in regularly, spot warning signs early, and show up to every touchpoint prepared. Use the practices in this guide as your operational baseline.

## Weekly account health check

Run through these four areas every week for each of your accounts. Schedule a fixed slot in your calendar — Friday mornings work well — so it becomes a habit rather than an afterthought.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Order volume vs. target" icon="chart-line">
    Compare actual order volume this week against the customer's target or historical average. A dip of more than 20% week-on-week is worth a quick Slack message to the admin contact to understand what's changed.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Open support tickets in Gleap" icon="ticket">
    Review any open or recently closed Gleap tickets for the account. Check resolution time and whether the customer acknowledged the fix. Tickets left open for more than 48 hours need your direct attention.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Complaints in Slack" icon="message-exclamation">
    Scan the customer's Slack channel for any complaints, frustrations, or unresolved questions. A message that didn't get a reply within two hours from anyone on your team is a risk to the relationship.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Upcoming renewals" icon="calendar-check">
    Flag any accounts with contract renewals in the next 60 days. Start the renewal conversation at least six weeks out — waiting until the last minute puts you at a disadvantage.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Communication cadence

Consistent communication prevents the silence that erodes customer trust. Follow this cadence for every active account.

| Frequency | Activity | Channel |
|---|---|---|
| Weekly | Order summary email | Email |
| Bi-weekly | Check-in call | Video call |
| Monthly | Business review | Slides (shared in advance) |
| As needed | Issue resolution | Slack + Gleap |
| Quarterly | Strategic account review | In-person or video call |

Adapt the cadence based on account size and maturity. New accounts in their first 90 days should receive more frequent touchpoints until they're fully self-sufficient on the platform.

## Identifying at-risk accounts

Catching an at-risk account early gives you time to intervene. Look for these warning signs during your weekly health check.

<Warning>
  If you spot two or more of these signals in the same account in the same week, escalate to your Team Lead immediately. Don't try to manage a deteriorating account alone.
</Warning>

- **Declining order frequency** — The account has placed fewer orders than usual for two or more consecutive weeks without a clear explanation (e.g., company holiday or office closure).
- **Multiple open Gleap tickets** — More than three unresolved support tickets in a 14-day window suggests systemic friction, not a one-off issue.
- **No response to check-ins** — The admin contact has not responded to two or more consecutive emails or calls. Silence from a previously engaged customer is a red flag.
- **Complaints escalated beyond KAM level** — If a customer has raised an issue directly with your manager or another team, it means they felt unheard at the KAM level.
- **Requests to pause orders** — A request to "pause for a few weeks" often signals internal budget pressure or dissatisfaction. Treat it as an at-risk signal and book a call to understand the reason.

## Upsell and expansion opportunities

Your existing accounts are your best source of growth. You don't need to pitch — you need to listen and observe. These signals often indicate an upsell or expansion opportunity.

**Signals to look for:**
- Consistently hitting or exceeding their monthly order volume cap
- Requests in Slack or on calls for cuisine types or catering styles not currently in their setup
- Mentions of new office locations, team expansions, or upcoming events
- Positive feedback about caterer quality paired with questions about variety

**What to do when you spot an opportunity:**
1. Log the opportunity in the CRM with relevant context (what was said, when, and by whom).
2. Brief your manager in your next one-to-one so they're aware before you move forward.
3. Tailor a short proposal — one page or a brief deck — that connects the expansion to the customer's specific needs. Don't send a generic upsell template.
4. Bring the proposal to your next scheduled business review rather than raising it cold over email.

<Tip>
  Use Claude Teams to summarize an account's recent Gleap messages and Slack activity before a business review call. It saves preparation time and helps you walk in with a clear picture of what's been happening across the account.
</Tip>
